For ADHD brains and everyone else

A note as a line of connected points

Dotline is not a to-do list. It is a sequence of steps where each next step is tied to the previous one — and, when needed, to an exact time.

Open Dotline

Why it works

1

Your head does not have to hold the whole chain

Working memory is the brain's scratchpad where you hold and rearrange steps while acting. In ADHD, its capacity is reduced: a meta-analysis of 26 studies found a stable deficit, strongest in the active part — the central executive (spatial component, effect size ≈ 1.06). In plain words: "keep all 6 steps and their order in my head" is exactly the operation that breaks first.

Dotline: the line stores the order for you. At any moment you only look at the next point — not the whole chain.

Martinussen et al. 2005 · Barkley 1997

2

The hard part is not "what to do" — it is "in what order"

In the same meta-analysis, manipulating information (reordering, sequencing) was more impaired than simple storage. In Barkley's theory this is "reconstitution" — the ability to break a task into parts and assemble them in the right order. This is ADHD's weak link. The person knows all the items, but reconstructing their order every time is expensive.

Dotline: the order is baked into the line itself. Items cannot be misplaced — they physically follow one another.

Martinussen et al. 2005 · Barkley 1997

3

Starting costs more than it seems — so steps are microscopic

PET studies showed reduced dopamine reward-pathway function (midbrain and nucleus accumbens) in adults with ADHD, and the reduction correlated with inattention and motivation deficit. The practical takeaway: the brain struggles to generate "activation energy" for the start — especially if the task is large, vague, or rewards come later. Procrastination here is not laziness; it is a fuel shortage at launch.

Dotline: every item is a micro-step ("leave the house," not "get ready for the flight"). Concrete, unambiguous, launchable right now.

Volkow et al. 2009 · Volkow et al. 2011

4

A future deadline does not feel urgent — so we count backward

Time perception is impaired in ADHD. A meta-analysis of 27 studies (1,620 ADHD participants vs. 1,249 controls) found time is perceived less accurately (Hedges' g > 0.40) and less stably (g = 0.66), with a tendency to overestimate duration. Barkley describes this as a problem of self-regulation over time: a distant deadline like "flight at 3:00 PM" stays abstract and creates no urgency until the last minute. "Start earlier" fails because the brain cannot switch into the right state until time becomes concrete.

Dotline: backward counting turns one abstract deadline into a chain of concrete ones. At the top is "flight 3:00 PM," below it "−1 h drive," and the second-to-last item is "leave the house at 2:00 PM." Each step carries its own local urgency.

Zheng et al. 2022 · Barkley 1997

5

A step tied to a moment almost launches itself

"Implementation intentions" are pre-formed rules of the form "if situation X (or time T) occurs, then I do action Y." They move control from willpower (top-down, requires executive functions) to an automatic response to a cue (bottom-up). In controlled experiments, these if-then plans raised impulse control and task adherence in children with ADHD to the level of neurotypical peers.

Dotline: a timed item is a ready-made implementation intention. "Leave the house at 2:00 PM" = "when it is 2:00 PM, then I leave." The service makes you write steps in exactly this form.

Gollwitzer 1999 · Gawrilow & Gollwitzer 2008 · Gawrilow et al. 2011

6

The closer the finish, the easier it gets — so progress is visible

The goal-gradient effect: effort accelerates as the reward approaches. In a field experiment with coffee cards ("10 purchases — 11th free"), people bought coffee more often the closer the reward was; the roots go back to Hull's animal maze experiments. Visible nearness of the finish itself pushes you forward.

Dotline: completed dots are filled in, the remaining line shrinks. You see how little is left — and every closed dot pulls you to the next.

Kivetz et al. 2006 · Hull 1932

7

"What time should I leave?" — a calculation that does not add up in your head

In reverse-countdown mode you do not set a time for every item by hand. You only set the deadline at the top ("flight 3:30 PM") and the duration of each gap between steps — packing 1 h 30 min, taxi to airport 1 h, buffer/wait 2 h. Dotline subtracts them one by one and fills in the exact time for each item down the line, all the way to the bottom one: "start packing — 11:00 AM."

Why this breaks in the head. Calculating "what time to leave" is a multi-step operation: hold the deadline, subtract each interval, remember the running total, do not lose track. This is exactly the working-memory manipulation that is weakest in ADHD (see block 1), layered on top of unreliable time perception (block 4). So the needed start time either does not get calculated at all, or gets calculated wrong — and the person leaves late "even though I thought I had it figured out."

A second effect is the planning fallacy. People systematically underestimate how long a whole task will take (Buehler, Griffin & Ross 1994). The reason: when estimating a large task, we do not break it into parts. Once a task is "unpacked" into sub-steps and each is estimated separately, the total estimate becomes markedly more accurate — this is the direct experimental result of Kruger & Evans (2004). Guessing "packing — 1.5 h" and "taxi — 1 h" is more honest and easier than guessing one number for "when to leave for the flight."

Dotline: you enter only durations — the service turns them into a chain of exact start moments. One abstract "flight 3:30 PM" unfolds into concrete "leave at 12:30 PM" and "start packing at 11:00 AM." Then each such moment works as a trigger signal that launches the step on its own (block 5).

Kruger & Evans 2004 · Buehler, Griffin & Ross 1994
